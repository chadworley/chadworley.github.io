  1. Question

    In a vacuum, electromagnetic radiation propagates at the speed of light: \(3.00 \cdot 10^{8}\) meters per second. In Earth’s air, the speed of light is almost as fast, and using three significant digits, the speed of light in Earth’s air is also \(3.00 \cdot 10^{8}\) meters per second. (Water slows light down to about \(2.25 \cdot 10^{8}\) meters per second.)

    A laser produces light at a single frequency (or at least a very narrow band) of light. When a laser outputs light at \(5.02\cdot 10^{14}\) Hz, the “color” is approximately (very approximately) the color shown below:


    Assuming that light travels through air, what is the wavelength in nanometers?


    Solution

    We can find the wavelength by dividing the velocity of light (\(c\)) by the frequency (\(f\)).

    \[\lambda = \frac{c}{f}\]

    Notice you could use dimensional analysis to help confirm the relationship. Remember, hertz are cycles per second, or in SI units, simply \(\mathrm{s^{-1}}\).

    \[\left(\mathrm{\frac{meter}{cycle}}\right)~=~\frac{\left(\mathrm{\frac{meter}{second}}\right)}{\left(\mathrm{\frac{cycle}{second}}\right)}\]

    In scientific notation,

    \[\lambda~=~\frac{3.00\cdot 10^8~\mathrm{m/s}}{5.02\cdot 10^{14}~\mathrm{1/s}}~\approx~5.98\cdot 10^{-7}~\mathrm{m}\]

    One nanometer equals \(10^{-9}\) meters, so we can rewrite the number: \(\lambda\approx 598\cdot10^{-9}\) meters which is approximately 598 nanometers. You could have also used a conversion factor based on the fact that \(10^9\) nanometers equals \(1\) meter.

    \[\lambda\approx~5.98\cdot 10^{-7}~\mathrm{m} \cdot \frac{10^9~\mathrm{nm}}{1~\mathrm{m}}~=~598~\mathrm{nm}\]
