30_decibels_from_power


  1. Question

    I find it interesting that it seems our brains subconsciously use logarithmic mapping to form our perceptions of many stimuli. Just like in pitch, where every doubling of frequency is felt as an equivalent increase of pitch (an octave), every doubling of sound power is felt as an even step in volume.

    We already saw that it is common to use cents when discussing pitch intervals. When discussing volume, we often use decibels.

    A common reference value of \(10^{-12}\) watts is considered a very low-powered sound, barely audible, less than the quietest breathing of a human. By setting a reference value, we can use the following logarithmic formula to find the “loudness” (\(L\)) in decibels of a sound with a known power, \(P\), in watts.

    \[L ~=~ 10 \cdot \log_{10}\left(\frac{P}{10^{-12}}\right)\]

    If the power is \(P=2.53\cdot 10^{-5}\) watts, what is the loudness, as defined above, in decibels?


    Solution

    Just put the number into the formula.

    \[L ~=~ 10 \cdot \log_{10}\left(\frac{2.53\cdot 10^{-5}}{10^{-12}}\right)\]

    \[L ~\approx ~ 74.0312052\]
