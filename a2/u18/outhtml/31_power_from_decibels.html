31_power_from_decibels


  1. Question

    I find it interesting that it seems our brains subconsciously use logarithmic mapping to form our perceptions of many stimuli. Just like in pitch, where every doubling of frequency is felt as an equivalent increase of pitch (an octave), every doubling of sound power is felt as an even step in volume.

    We already saw that it is common to use cents when discussing pitch intervals. When discussing volume, we often use decibels.

    A common reference value of \(10^{-12}\) watts is considered a very low-powered sound, barely audible, less than the quietest breathing of a human. By setting a reference value, we can use the following logarithmic formula to find the “loudness” (\(L\)) in decibels of a sound with a known power, \(P\), in watts.

    \[L ~=~ 10 \cdot \log_{10}\left(\frac{P}{10^{-12}}\right)\]

    If the loudness, as defined above, is \(L=56.7\) decibels, what is the power of the sound source in watts?


    1. \(4.68\cdot 10^{-8}\) watts
    2. \(4.68\cdot 10^{-7}\) watts
    3. \(4.68\cdot 10^{-6}\) watts
    4. \(4.68\cdot 10^{-5}\) watts
    5. \(4.70\cdot 10^{-8}\) watts
    6. \(4.70\cdot 10^{-7}\) watts
    7. \(4.70\cdot 10^{-6}\) watts
    8. \(4.70\cdot 10^{-5}\) watts
    9. \(4.72\cdot 10^{-8}\) watts
    10. \(4.72\cdot 10^{-7}\) watts
    11. \(4.72\cdot 10^{-6}\) watts
    12. \(4.72\cdot 10^{-5}\) watts
    13. \(4.74\cdot 10^{-8}\) watts
    14. \(4.74\cdot 10^{-7}\) watts
    15. \(4.74\cdot 10^{-6}\) watts
    16. \(4.74\cdot 10^{-5}\) watts

    Solution

    \[56.7 ~=~ 10 \cdot \log_{10}\left(\frac{P}{10^{-12}}\right)\]

    Divide by 10. Notice, the decibels divided by 10 is the bels.

    \[5.67 ~=~ \log_{10}\left(\frac{P}{10^{-12}}\right)\]

    Use exponentiation to undo the logarithmic function.

    \[10^{5.67} ~=~ \frac{P}{10^{-12}}\]

    Multiply by \(10^{-12}\).

    \[10^{5.67}\cdot 10^{-12} ~=~ P\]

    \[10^{5.67-12} ~=~ P\]

    \[10^{-6.33} ~=~ P\]

    \[P~=~4.68\cdot 10^{-7}~\mathrm{watts}\]
