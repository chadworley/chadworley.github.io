26_pH


  1. Question

    In chemistry, when making aqueous solutions with acids and bases, it is common to consider the pH. Distilled (pure) water at 25°C has a pH of 7, and it is neutral because the number of \(\mathrm{H^+}\) ions equals the number of \(\mathrm{OH^-}\) ions. If an acid is added to water, the pH decreases, the number of \(\mathrm{H^+}\) ions increases, and the number of \(\mathrm{OH^-}\) ions decreases. If a base is added to water, the pH increases, the number of \(\mathrm{H^+}\) ions decreases, and the number of \(\mathrm{OH^-}\) ions increases.

    At room temperature, water undergoes a reversible reaction dictated by collision theory called auto-ionization of water. At each moment, every water molecule has some chance to split into a \(\mathrm{H^+}\) and a \(\mathrm{OH^-}\). Also at each moment, if a \(\mathrm{H^+}\) bumps into a \(\mathrm{OH^-}\), they can reform a water molecule.

    The bajillions of molecules all taking part in this dance produce a stable equilibrium, described by an equilibrium constant.

    \[\mathrm{\left[H^+\right]\cdot\left[OH^-\right]}~=~10^{-14}\]

    where \(\mathrm{\left[H^+\right]}\) is the concentration in moles per liter of \(\mathrm{H^+}\) and \(\mathrm{\left[OH^-\right]}\) is the concentration in moles per liter of \(\mathrm{OH^-}\). The equilibrium constant equation shown above holds true for all aqueous solutions (at room temperature, near 1 bar of atmospheric pressure, and not too concentrated), even after acids or bases are added.

    In typical aqueous solutions used in laboratories, the concentration of \(\mathrm{H^+}\) varies considerably, over many magnitudes, from about 10 moles per liter down to 0.000000000000001 moles per liter. It is annoying to work with these numbers with lots of place-holder 0s. Using scientific notation can help, but using a logarithmic scale has been a favorite strategy when dealing with numbers that vary over many orders of magnitude. This is what pH is, a logarithmic representation of the concentration of hydrogen ions. In the laboratory, pH will be between -1 and 15.

    \[\mathrm{pH~=~-\log_{10}\left(\left[H^+\right]\right)}\]

    • If an aqueous solution has known concentration of hydrogen ions, \(\mathrm{\left[H^+\right]}=2.51\cdot 10^{-2}\) moles per liter, what is the pH? The tolerance is \(\pm 0.1\).

    Solution

    Plug the given number into the equation.

    \[\mathrm{pH~=~-\log_{10}\left(2.51\cdot 10^{-2}\right)}\]

    \[\mathrm{pH}~=~1.6\]
