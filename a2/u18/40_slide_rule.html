Question

Historically, logarithmic transformations were used to help people multiply many-digit numbers. In fact, when John Napier published books filled with logarithmic mappings, he did not even know that logarithmic functions are inverses of exponential functions. His sole purpose, when publishing his discovery of logarithmic mapping, was to aid in multiplication.

The relevant logarithmic identity is that the log of a product is equivalent to a sum of logs:

\[\log_{b}(x\cdot y)~\equiv~\log_{b}(x)+\log_b(y)\]

So, instead of directly calculating \(x\cdot y\), a ship’s navigator could look up \(\log(x)\) and \(\log(y)\), add those two numbers, and reverse look up from the resulting \(\log(xy)\) to find \(xy\). For example, imagine a navigator wanted to multiply \(2.34\) and \(7.65\). In that table, they could find \(\log(2.34)\approx 1.2265\) and \(\log(7.65)\approx 2.9355\). Now, it is relatively easy to add those two results: \(1.2265+2.9355=4.1620\). Lastly, the navigator could find the row in the book that says \(\log(17.9)\approx 4.1620\). So, then the navigator could conclude that \(2.34\times 7.65 \approx 17.9\). This process was actually useful.
