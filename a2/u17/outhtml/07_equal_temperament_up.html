07_equal_temperament_up


  1. Question

    Most modern music is written in a twelve-tone equal temperament system. Thus, when playing an ascending chromatic scale, every new note’s fundamental frequency is approximately \(5.9463094\)% higher than the previous note’s.

    Typically, the A above middle C (noted as \(A_4\) in scientific pitch notation) is tuned to a frequency of 440 Hz, but throughout history this was not so standardized.

    Let’s imagine an orchestra tunes \(A_4\) to 443 Hz. What will the frequency be of the \(C^{\#}_5\) that is 4 notes higher than \(A_4\)?


    Solution

    Find the scale factor.

    \[1+0.0594631~=~1.0594631\]

    The exact scale factor for twelve-tone equal temperament is \(2^{1/12}\).

    You multiply the given frequency by the scale factor 4 times.

    \[443\cdot 1.0594631^{4} ~=~ 558.1450251\]

    Some composers (including BART legend James Bergin), compose in other equal temperaments. Some common ones include 5, 7, 9, 15, 17, 19, 22, 23, 24, 31, 34, 41, 53, 58, 72, and 96 (I believe Mr. Bergin is partial to 72-EDO). In a 72-tone equal temperament, two adjacent notes have a scale factor of \(2^{1/72}\approx 1.0096735\), so as you play a chromatic scale, the frequency increases by 0.9673533% for each note.
