04_arc_len_from_unit_arc


  1. Question

    Two concentric circles have radii \(r_1=1\) meters and \(r_2=3.4\) meters. A central angle subtends similar arcs on both circles: a blue arc on the smaller circle and a red arc on the larger circle. The blue arc length is 1.95 meters, as shown in the diagram below.


    What is the length, in meters, of the red arc? The tolerance is set to \(\pm0.01\) meters.


    Solution

    Okay, this might seem absurdly easy if you are comfortable with similarity.

    \[\frac{1}{1.95} ~=~ \frac{3.4}{?}\]

    \[?~=~ 1.95\cdot 3.4 ~=~ 6.63\]

    I am trying to introduce the radian, which is the arc length of a unit circle.

    • Massachusetts learning objective F-TF.A.1:
      • Understand radian measure of an angle as the length of the arc on the unit circle subtended by the angle.

    In this case, the central angle is 112, which is approximately equivalent to 1.95 radians. To convert between degrees and radians, we can multiply by \(\frac{\pi}{180^{\circ}}\) to go from degrees to radians or multiply by \(\frac{180^{\circ}}{\pi}\) to convert from radians to degrees.

    As you progress in math, youll use radians by default, not degrees. One of the best reasons for this depends on concepts learned in calculus but in short if we use radians, then the derivative of \(\sin(x)\) is simply \(\cos(x)\). If we used degrees, then calculus with trigonometric functions would be more annoying.

    If you look up circular arc on wikipedia, youll see a formula relating the arc length (\(L\)) to radius (\(r\)) and the central angle measured in radians (\(\theta\)). \[L~=~r\theta\]

    So, in our case:

    \[L~=~(3.4)(1.95)\]

    \[L~=~6.63~\text{m}\]
