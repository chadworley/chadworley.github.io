  1. Question

    A circle with radius \(R=4\) ft is cut to a sector with an angle of \(\theta=\frac{7\pi}{6}\) radians.


    By connecting the initial side and terminal side, the sector is bent (in 3 dimensions) into a right-circular cone with height \(h\) and radius \(r\). Notice the cone’s slant height equals the radius of the original circle (\(R\)) and the circumference of the cone matches the arc length (\(L\)) of the original circle.


    Find the ratio of the cone’s height to the cone’s radius.

    \[\frac{h}{r} ~=~ ?\]

    The tolerance is \(\pm 0.01\).


    Solution

    First, find the arc length.

    \[L ~=~ \frac{7\pi}{6} \cdot 4 ~=~ \frac{14\pi}{3}\]

    We know that a circle’s circumference is twice its radius times \(\pi\). Look at the circular base of the cone, and use the circumference formula to find its radius. Remember \(L\) is the circumference of the cone’s base.

    \[L=2\pi r\]

    Divide both sides by \(2\pi\) and switch sides to get \(r=\frac{L}{2\pi}\).

    \[r ~=~ \frac{L}{2\pi} ~=~ \frac{\frac{14\pi}{3}}{2\pi}\]

    \[r ~=~ \frac{7}{3}\]

    To get the height of the cone, use Pythagorean theorem, which states a right triangle with legs \(a\) and \(b\) and hypotenuse \(c\) is constrained by \(a^2+b^2=c^2\).

    \[\left(\frac{7}{3}\right)^2 + h^2 = (4)^2\]

    Solve for \(h\).

    \[h = \sqrt{(4)^2-\left(\frac{7}{3}\right)^2}\]

    \[h\approx 3.2489314\]

    Find the ratio.

    \[\frac{h}{r} ~=~ \frac{\sqrt{(4)^2-\left(\frac{7}{3}\right)^2}}{\frac{7}{3}}\]

    \[\frac{h}{r} \approx 1.3923992\]

    Fancy stuff

    With some thought, you might convince yourself that this ratio \(\frac{h}{r}\) should be independent of the radius of the original circle. For example, if you measured the radius \(R\) in inches (instead of feet), the \(\frac{h}{r}\) ratio shouldn’t change. Or if you doubled \(R\), it seems \(\frac{h}{r}\) should be the same.

    By approaching this problem more algebraically, you might reach some interesting insights. For example, if we define \(f\) to be the fraction of the original circle.

    \[f = \frac{\theta}{2\pi}\]

    You can show that the ratio of radii also equals \(f\).

    \[\frac{r}{R} = f\]

    Substituting \(r=Rf\), you can generalize the formula to find \(h\) in terms of \(R\) and \(f\): \[h ~=~ \sqrt{R^2-r^2} ~=~ \sqrt{R^2-(fR)^2} ~=~ R\sqrt{1-f^2}\]

    Using that information, you can derive a simple formula for \(\frac{h}{r}\) that only depends on \(f\).
