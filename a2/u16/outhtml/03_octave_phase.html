  1. Question

    In additive synthesis, a musician combines pure tones by adding together sine waves to produce a large variety of tones.

    In harmonic additive synthesis, a sine wave with a fundamental frequency is added to sine waves with positive-integer multiples of the fundamental frequency. Each harmonic has its own amplitude and phase shift. The exact set of frequencies, their amplitudes, and their phases dictates the timbre of the musical tone.

    For example, the musician might combine a sine wave with amplitude of 1 and frequency of 100 Hz with another sine wave with amplitude 1 and frequency of 200 Hz. In Desmos, you can generate this tone with tone([100,200],[1,1]).

    Interestingly, Desmos does not allow us to control the phase of the component waves when using the tone function. In most cases, the psychoacoustic perception (of human hearing) wonâ€™t distinguish between various phase shifts. However, we can see some striking visual changes to the waveform when we change the initial phase of a component.

    For example, in Desmos, type the following equation, using a slider for \(c\).

    \[y~~=~~\sin(2\pi100 t)~+~\sin(2\pi 200 t+c)\]

    The slider \(c\) represents a horizontal shift in the 200-Hz wave. Find the (approximate) value of \(c\), such that \(0\le c < 2\pi\) to achieve the waveform shown below:


    Notice, youâ€™ll need to selectively zoom in on the horizontal axis.


    Solution

    1. Type y = sin(2 pi 100 t) + sin(2 pi 200 t + c) into Desmos, and add a slider for \(c\) (but not \(t\)).
    2. Click the Graph Settings button (which looks like a wrench in the top-right corner of the graph).
    3. Change the \(x\) axis to have limits so that \(0\le x \le 0.03\).
    4. Change the \(y\) axis to have limits so that \(-2\le x \le 2\).
    5. Adjust slider \(c\) until the waveform matches the promptâ€™s graph. I find it helpful to look at the \(y\)-coordinates of the peaks and troughs.

    The exact value of \(c\) was 0.6648264, but the tolerance was \(\pm 0.1\), so you didnâ€™t have to be super precise.
