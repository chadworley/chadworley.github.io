23_round_trip_ave_speed


  1. Question

    An alien creature flies a round trip — from home, to school, and back home — using the exact same route both directions (so the distances of the initial and return trips are equal).

    On the initial trip, the creature maintained a speed of 78 meters per second. On the return trip, the creature maintained a speed of 49 meters per second.

    What was the average speed, in m/s, of the entire trip? (The tolerance is \(\pm 0.01\) m/s.)


    Solution

    You might incorrectly guess the average speed for the trip is simply \(\frac{78+49}{2}\), but you’d be wrong.

    The “easy” way to do this is to pick a distance. You can later check that you’ll get the same answer for any distance.

    I’ll pick a distance of 1000 meters for each trip.

    • Time of initial trip = \(\frac{1000}{78}=12.8205128\) seconds.
    • Time of return trip = \(\frac{1000}{49}=20.4081633\) seconds.

    The total distance traveled is 2000 meters. The total time is \(12.8205128+20.4081633=33.2286761\) seconds. To find the average speed, divide the total distance by the total time.

    \[\frac{2000~\mathrm{m}}{33.2286761~\mathrm{s}}~=~60.1889764~~ \mathrm{\frac{m}{s}}\]

    Of course, there is a cooler way to do this. Let’s use the following variables:

    \[\begin{align} s_1 &= \text{speed to school}\\ s_2 &= \text{speed returning home}\\ D &= \text{distance}\\ t_1 &= \text{time to get to school}\\ t_2 &= \text{time to return home}\\ \bar{s} &= \text{average speed for round trip} \end{align}\]

    We can make some simple statements:

    \[\bar{s} = \frac{D}{t_1+t_2}\] \[t_1 = \frac{D}{s_1}\] \[t_2 = \frac{D}{s_2}\]

    If you combine all three of the above equations, you should be able to make a formula for \(\bar{s}\) that only depends on \(s_1\) and \(s_2\).
