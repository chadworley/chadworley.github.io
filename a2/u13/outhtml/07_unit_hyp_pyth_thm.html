  1. Question

    A right triangle is drawn with a hypotenuse of length 1 and a leg of length \(\frac{7\sqrt{14}}{31}\). The other leg is of length \(x\).


    The other leg can be expressed as a fraction of a radical.

    \[x~=~\frac{a\sqrt{b}}{c}\]

    where \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) are positive integers. Find the length of the other leg. Express the length exactly by stating the values of \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\).

    • \(a=\)
    • \(b=\)
    • \(c=\)


    Solution

    Use the Pythagorean Theorem.

    \[\left(\frac{7\sqrt{14}}{31}\right)^2+x^2~=~1^2\]

    When a product (or quotient) is raised to a power, the power can be distributed to the factors. If there was addition (or subtraction) in the fraction, then this would be more complicated. Also, \(1^2=1\).

    \[\frac{7^2\cdot 14}{31^2}+x^2~=~1\]

    Evaluate the numerator and denominator.

    \[\frac{686}{961}+x^2~=~1\]

    Subtract \(\frac{686}{961}\) from both sides.

    \[x^2~=~1-\frac{686}{961}\]

    Get a common denominator by recognizing that \(1=\frac{961}{961}\).

    \[x^2~=~\frac{961}{961}-\frac{686}{961}\]

    In general, \(\frac{m}{d}-\frac{n}{d}\equiv \frac{m-n}{d}\), for any \(m,n,d\); that’s the reason we got a common denominator in the first place.

    \[x^2~=~\frac{961-686}{961}\]

    Simplify the numerator.

    \[x^2~=~\frac{275}{961}\]

    Take the square root of both sides. We are confident that we are looking for a positive value of \(x\), so we do not need to consider the negative square root.

    \[x~=~\sqrt{\frac{275}{961}}\]

    Since a square-root radical is equivalent to raising to the power of 1/2, we can again distribute this power to the factors of the quotient.

    \[x~=~\frac{\sqrt{275}}{\sqrt{961}}\]

    The denominator is easy to simplify, because \(\sqrt{961}=31\).

    \[x~=~\frac{\sqrt{275}}{31}\]

    The numerator is a bit trickier to simplify. We need to find the largest perfect-square factor of 275. Personally, I’d do this by making a factor tree using an algorithm based on the simplest primality test, which involves using trial division. Specifically, in this case, I’d start by trying to divide 275 by all primes less than \(\sqrt{275}\approx 16.583124\). So, I’d try to divide 275 by all the numbers in this list: 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13.

    After a bit of work, you could find that 5 is a factor of 275.

    \[x~=~\frac{\sqrt{5\cdot55}}{31}\]

    We can now repeat that strategy with the non-prime factor until we have only prime factors.

    \[x=\frac{\sqrt{ 5\cdot5 \cdot 11 }}{ 31 }\]

    Look for factors that repeat. Again, we can treat a square-root radical like a power, so since \((fg)^k\equiv f^kg^k\), we can split up the square root of a product into a product of square roots.

    \[\frac{\sqrt{5\cdot5}\cdot\sqrt{11}}{31}\]

    Simplify.

    \[\frac{5\sqrt{11}}{31}\]

    So,

    • \(a=5\)
    • \(b=11\)
    • \(c=31\)
