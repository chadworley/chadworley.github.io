  1. Question

    At sunset, the sunâ€™s rays are parallel to the local flat ground. A rocket is launched at an angle of elevation of 0.969 radians above the horizon at a speed of 4150 meters per second. Its shadow is projected onto a tall cliff to the east.


    How fast, in meters per second, is the shadow moving up the cliff? The tolerance is \(\pm 10\) m/s.


    Solution

    We recognize that velocity is a vector. In this scenario, we can think of the vector as a 2D vector given in polar form.

    Using basic right-triangle trigonometry, you can find the horizontal component of the velocity.

    Draw a vector with magnitude (length) of 4150 m/s and angle of 0.969 radians. We can indicate the magnitude with double vertical lines on either side of the vectorâ€™s name. We can also indicate magnitude by leaving off the arrow.

    \[||\vec{v}||~=~ v ~=~4150~\mathrm{\frac{m}{s}}\]

    We want to decompose the vector into its horizontal component (\(\vec{v}_x\)) and its vertical component (\(\vec{v}_y\)). The vertical componentâ€™s magnitude (denoted as \(||\vec{v}_y||\), or more simply as \(v_y\)) is the speed of the shadow.


    We can set up the sine equation.

    \[\sin(0.969)~=~\frac{v_y}{4150}\]

    Solve for \(v_y\).

    \[v_y~=~4150\cdot\sin(0.969)\]

    Evaluate with a calculator. Remember, we are in RADIAN MODE!

    \[v_y~=~3420.9280061\] \[v_y~\approx~3420\]
