  1. Question

    • Learning objective S-ID.A.4: Use the mean and standard deviation of a data set to fit it to a normal distribution and to estimate population percentages. Recognize that there are data sets for which such a procedure is not appropriate. Use calculators, spreadsheets, and tables to estimate areas under the normal curve.

    On an alien planet, you harvested a sample of adult red-legged vultures. You measured their lengths, in centimeters; those measurements are shown below.

    Length (cm)
    104.0
    93.6
    84.2
    94.9
    97.0
    95.6
    88.4
    103.3
    94.1


    Your boss asks you to use a normal approximation of the population to estimate the proportion of adult red-legged vultures with lengths less than 89.1 centimeters.

    You say, “But we don’t know the lengths are normally distributed. They could be bimodal, uniform, skewed, or even non-normal-bell shaped. It does not seem appropriate to just assume the lengths of red-legged vultures are normally distributed.”

    Your boss mumbles something about genetics and alleles and how the length is determined by a multitude of factors. She tells you to just do it. You don’t find it super compelling, but then you remember she is your boss. Also, you figure you can at least state the assumptions clearly in the report. Besides, you know that there is a long history of assuming features like lengths and weights are normally distributed.

    To do the normal approximation:

    1. Calculate the sample mean and sample standard deviation.
    2. Assume those sample statistics (\(\bar{x}\) and \(s\)) are good estimates of the corresponding normal-curve parameters (\(\mu\) and \(\sigma\)).
    3. Find the area under the normal curve between \(-\infty\) and 89.1.
    • Approximately what proportion of red-legged vultures have lengths less than 89.1 centimeters?

    Solution

    The solution (if you do not round anything until the end) is 0.17, which expressed as a percentage is 17%.

    \[P(X<89.1) ~=~ 0.1739 ~=~ 17.39\%\]

    First, find the sample statistics:

    • \(\bar{x} = 95.0111111\)
    • \(s=6.2971113\)

    Then, find the area under a normal curve with parameters \(\mu=95.0111111\) and \(\sigma=6.2971113\) between \(-\infty\) and 89.1.

    Personally, I’d set up a spreadsheet to do this for me. To find normal-curve areas, you can use the NORM.DIST function.

    There is a concept that is worth reviewing first. A cumulative distribution function tells you the probability of the distribution producing a value less than (or equal to) a specified boundary. We referred to this as a left area when we found normal probabilities. For example, let \(X\) represent a normal random variable with \(\mu=95.0111111\) and \(\sigma=6.2971113\).

    \[X \sim N(95.01, 6.30)\]

    We can find the left area (the cumulative probability) using =NORM.DIST(89.1, 95.0111111, 6.2971113, TRUE).

    \[P(X\le 89.1) = 0.1739\]

    plot of chunk unnamed-chunk-1


    Spreadsheet

    To find normal-curve area in a spreadsheet, use the NORM.DIST function. This function takes four arguments:

    • \(x\): An upper boundary of the area
    • mean: The normal-curve mean (\(\mu\))
    • standard_deviation: The normal-curve standard deviation (\(\sigma\))
    • cumulative: A boolean set to TRUE to find an area. (If it is set to FALSE, then we get the height of the curve at \(x\), but that is not super useful except to graph the curve.)

    The NORM.DIST function returns the area under the normal curve from \(-\infty\) to the given boundary.

    A B C D
    1 length (cm) xbar (sample mean) b (boundary) P(X<b)
    2 104.00 =AVERAGE(A:A) 89.1 =NORM.DIST(C2,B2,B5,TRUE)
    3 93.60
    4 84.20 s (sample standard deviation)
    5 94.90 =STDEV(A:A)
    6 97.00
    7 95.60
    8 88.40

    \[P(X<49.7) ~=~ 0.8011 ~=~ 80.11\%\]

    First, find the sample statistics:

    • \(\bar{x} = 45.11\)
    • \(s=5.4280035\)

    Then, find the area under a normal curve with parameters \(\mu=45.11\) and \(\sigma=5.4280035\) between \(-\infty\) and 49.7.

    Personally, I’d set up a spreadsheet to do this for me. To find normal-curve areas, you can use the NORM.DIST function.

    There is a concept that is worth reviewing first. A cumulative distribution function tells you the probability of the distribution producing a value less than (or equal to) a specified boundary. We referred to this as a left area when we found normal probabilities. For example, let \(X\) represent a normal random variable with \(\mu=45.11\) and \(\sigma=5.4280035\).

    \[X \sim N(45.11, 5.43)\]

    We can find the left area (the cumulative probability) using =NORM.DIST(49.7, 45.11, 5.4280035, TRUE).

    \[P(X\le 49.7) = 0.8011\]

    Spreadsheet

    To find normal-curve area in a spreadsheet, use the NORM.DIST function. This function takes four arguments:

    • \(x\): An upper boundary of the area
    • mean: The normal-curve mean (\(\mu\))
    • standard_deviation: The normal-curve standard deviation (\(\sigma\))
    • cumulative: A boolean set to TRUE to find an area. (If it is set to FALSE, then we get the height of the curve at \(x\), but that is not super useful except to graph the curve.)

    The NORM.DIST function returns the area under the normal curve from \(-\infty\) to the given boundary.
