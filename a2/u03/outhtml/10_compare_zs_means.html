  1. Question

    A farm produces 4 types of fruit: apples, pineapples, coconuts, and papayas. The fruitsâ€™ masses have population parameters dependent on the type of fruit. (All values are in grams.)

    Type of fruit \(\mu\), population mean mass in grams \(\sigma\), population standard deviation in grams
    apple 208 11
    pineapple 1617 176
    coconut 660 54
    papaya 545 77

    A sample of each type is weighed. The results are shown below.

    Type of fruit \(n\), sample size \(\bar{x}\), sample mean
    apple 49 210.5
    pineapple 121 1600.0
    coconut 25 639.7
    papaya 100 537.9

    For each sample, determine the standard score and the cumulative probability by assuming the requirements for the central limit theorem are met. Then determine which sample mean is most unusually large, most unusually small, most typically sized, and most unusually sized.

    The standard score of a sample mean: \[z = \frac{\bar{x}-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\]

    Some people prefer to call this denominator the standard error. \[\text{standard error of mean} = \text{SE} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \] So the standard score (\(z\)-score) of a sample mean can also be expressed as \[z = \frac{\bar{x}-\mu}{\text{SE}} \]

    1. Calculate a \(z\)-score for the apple sample mean. (Round to the nearest hundredth.)
    1. What proportion of apple samples of size \(n=49\) have a smaller mean than the apple sample?
    1. Calculate a \(z\)-score for the pineapple sample mean. (Round to the nearest hundredth.)
    1. What proportion of pineapple samples of size \(n=121\) have a smaller mean than the pineapple sample?
    1. Calculate a \(z\)-score for the coconut sample mean. (Round to the nearest hundredth.)
    1. What proportion of coconut samples of size \(n=25\) have a smaller mean than the coconut sample?
    1. Calculate a \(z\)-score for the papaya sample mean. (Round to the nearest hundredth.)
    1. What proportion of papaya samples of size \(n=100\) have a smaller mean than the papaya sample?
    1. Which sample mean is most unusually large?
    1. Which sample mean is most unusually small?
    1. Which sample mean is most typically sized?
    1. Which sample mean is most unusually sized?


    Solution

    The formula to determine the \(z\)-score of a sample mean is the ratio shown below.

    \[z = \frac{\bar{x}-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \]

    The highest \(z\)-score (furthest right on number line) corresponds to the most unusually large sample mean.

    The smallest \(z\)-score (furthest left on number line) corresponds to the most unusually small sample mean.

    The smallest absolute \(z\)-score corresponds to the most usually sized sample mean.

    The largest absolute \(z\)-score corresponds to the most unusually sized sample mean.

    Spreadsheet:

    By using the formulas, you should be able to get a spreadsheet like the one displayed here:


    • We use the formula for calculating the apple sample-mean \(z\) score. \[z = \frac{210.5-208}{11/\sqrt{49}} = 1.59 \]
    • Use the \(z\) table to find the cumulative probability. \[P(Z<1.59) = 0.94 \]
    • We use the formula for calculating the pineapple sample-mean \(z\) score. \[z = \frac{1600-1617}{176/\sqrt{121}} = -1.06 \]
    • Use the \(z\) table to find the cumulative probability. \[P(Z<-1.06) = 0.14 \]
    • We use the formula for calculating the coconut sample-mean \(z\) score. \[z = \frac{639.7-660}{54/\sqrt{25}} = -1.88 \]
    • Use the \(z\) table to find the cumulative probability. \[P(Z<-1.88) = 0.03 \]
    • We use the formula for calculating the papaya sample-mean \(z\) score. \[z = \frac{537.9-545}{77/\sqrt{100}} = -0.92 \]
    • Use the \(z\) table to find the cumulative probability. \[P(Z<-0.92) = 0.18 \]
    • We determine the maximum \(z\) score is \(1.59\), which belongs to the apple.
    • We determine the minimum \(z\) score is \(-1.88\), which belongs to the coconut.
    • We determine the minimum absolute \(z\) score is \(0.92\), which belongs to the papaya.
    • We determine the maximum absolute \(z\) score is \(1.88\), which belongs to the coconut.

    The standard score of a sample mean: \[z = \frac{\bar{x}-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}\]

    Some people prefer to call this denominator the standard error. \[\text{standard error of mean} = \text{SE} = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}} \] So the standard score (\(z\)-score) of a sample mean can also be expressed as \[z = \frac{\bar{x}-\mu}{\text{SE}} \]

    1. Calculate a \(z\)-score for the coconut sample mean. (Round to the nearest hundredth.)
    1. What proportion of coconut samples of size \(n=25\) have a smaller mean than the coconut sample?
    1. Calculate a \(z\)-score for the watermelon sample mean. (Round to the nearest hundredth.)
    1. What proportion of watermelon samples of size \(n=121\) have a smaller mean than the watermelon sample?
    1. Calculate a \(z\)-score for the apricot sample mean. (Round to the nearest hundredth.)
    1. What proportion of apricot samples of size \(n=144\) have a smaller mean than the apricot sample?
    1. Calculate a \(z\)-score for the banana sample mean. (Round to the nearest hundredth.)
    1. What proportion of banana samples of size \(n=100\) have a smaller mean than the banana sample?
    1. Which sample mean is most unusually large?
    1. Which sample mean is most unusually small?
    1. Which sample mean is most typically sized?
    1. Which sample mean is most unusually sized?


    Solution

    The formula to determine the \(z\)-score of a sample mean is the ratio shown below.

    \[z = \frac{\bar{x}-\mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \]

    The highest \(z\)-score (furthest right on number line) corresponds to the most unusually large sample mean.

    The smallest \(z\)-score (furthest left on number line) corresponds to the most unusually small sample mean.

    The smallest absolute \(z\)-score corresponds to the most usually sized sample mean.

    The largest absolute \(z\)-score corresponds to the most unusually sized sample mean.
