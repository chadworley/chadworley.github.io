  1. Question

    Let matrices \(A\) and \(B\) be defined below.

    \[A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 5 & 3\\ 4 & 9 & 1\\ 6 & 8 & 7 \end{bmatrix}\] \[B = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 3 & 9\\ 1 & 6 & 2\\ 7 & 8 & 5 \end{bmatrix}\]

    A third matrix, \(C\), is equal to the product \(AB\). A student set up a diagram to help find the product:


    What is \(c_{1,3}\)?


    Solution

    To find \(c_{1,3}\), you use row 1 from matrix \(A\) and column 3 from matrix \(B\).


    You multiply corresponding elements and sum those products.

    \[c_{1,3}~=~( 2 \cdot 9 )+( 5 \cdot 2 )+( 3 \cdot 5 )\]

    \[c_{1,3}~=~18+10+15\]

    \[c_{1,3}~=~43\]

    If you repeat this process for all 9 elements in \(C\), you get:

    \[\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 5 & 3\\ 4 & 9 & 1\\ 6 & 8 & 7 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 3 & 9\\ 1 & 6 & 2\\ 7 & 8 & 5 \end{bmatrix} ~=~ \begin{bmatrix} 34 & 60 & 43\\ 32 & 74 & 59\\ 81 & 122 & 105 \end{bmatrix}\]
