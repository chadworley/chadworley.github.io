  1. Question

    S-IC.A.2: Decide if a specified model is consistent with results from a given data-generating process, e.g., using simulation.

    You have heard from a good authority (pet food survey) that a few years ago a survey showed that in Berkshire County the proportion of cats that prefer wet food over dry food is \(p=0.66\). You think that maybe the population proportion has changed with the times. You decide to test your hunch by getting a simple random sample of the cats in Berkshire County.

    But, you don’t just start gathering data. You need to have a clear test in mind before gathering any data; otherwise you might change the test in response to the data (see post hoc analysis).

    Decide on test

    First, you decide on a sample size. You carefully balance your expectations of time, cost, and enthusiasm to decide on a sample size of \(n=24\).

    For your test, you decide on a significance level of 5%. You will use a simulation (with \(p=0.66\)) to find an interval of the middle 95% of outcomes. Then,

    • If the observed result is outside the interval, you will conclude the current proportion is not equal \(0.66\).
      • You will say that you “found a significant difference” between the old proportion and the new proportion.
    • Otherwise, if the observed result is inside the interval, you will conclude that there remain two possibilities:
      • The current proportion still equals 0.66.
      • You need more data to detect the difference.
      • In this case, you can just say you “did not find a significant difference”.

    Simulation

    With the sample size \(n=24\) in mind you are able to run a (repeated) simulation while assuming \(p=0.66\). To do that in a spreadsheet, each cell will use =IF(RAND()<0.66,1,0), which uses 1 for affirmative and 0 for negative.

    • The simulation shows 1 for a cat that prefers wet food
    • The simulation shows 0 for a cat that prefers dry food
    • To simulate checking 24 cats, use =IF(RAND()<0.66,1,0) in all cells in range A1:X1.
    • To count how many of the 24 cats are a cat that prefers wet food, in cell Z1, use =sum(A1:X1)
    • Repeat the simulation in rows 1 to 1000. Highlight A1:X1, ctrl+shift+down, and ctrl+enter.
    • To get result of each row, highlight Z1, ctrl+shift+down, and ctrl+enter.
    • Freeze the simulation with ctrl+a, ctrl+c, and ctrl+shift+v.
    • To sort, highlight column Z. Click Data, Sort Sheet by column Z.
    • Because you have 1000 repetitions, and \(0.025\cdot1000=25\), drop the lowest 25 and the highest 25 results. In other words, get the numbers in Z26 and Z975. Those two numbers indicate the interval of typical results.
