  1. Question

    S-IC.B.5: Use data from a randomized experiment to compare two treatments; use simulations to decide if differences between parameters are significant.

    Dante and Yara wonder if they are equally skilled at archery. They decide to compare this year’s scores using a two-tail approximate permutation test, using a significance level of 5%.

    Dante Yara
    224 200
    236 216
    235 245
    230 223
    236 207
    216 192
    210 204
    237 208
    239
    234
    226
    232



    To run the test, first calculate the observed absolute difference of sample means.

    • Dante’s sample mean = \(\bar{x}_1 =\)
    • Yara’s sample mean = \(\bar{x}_2 =\)
    • The absolute difference = \(|\bar{x}_2 - \bar{x}_1| =\)

    Permutation Test

    1. Reshuffle those two samples 1000 times. I’d recommend this tool, which wants comma-separated data, so here are the samples in that format:

      224, 236, 235, 230, 236, 216, 210, 237, 239, 234, 226, 232

      200, 216, 245, 223, 207, 192, 204, 208

      Notice this reshuffling is equivalent to putting all the scores on cards, shuffling them all together, and then dealing into two piles (repeated 1000 times). Notice, Dante had 12 scores, so one pile always gets 12 numbers, and the other pile gets 8 numbers. We are simulating the hypothesis that maybe both people’s sample came from equivalent populations with a difference of sample means from natural variation, not unequal skill. We will then see if the observed difference is copacetic with the simulated differences.

    2. Paste the 1000 rows into a spreadsheet.

    3. For each row, find the absolute difference of the means. Remember, spreadsheets have =ABS() for absolute value and =AVERAGE() to find a mean.

    4. How many times was the simulation’s absolute difference of means smaller than the observed absolute difference of means. (Choose an interval.)

    1. How many times was the simulation’s difference as big or bigger than the observation’s difference? (Choose an interval.)
    1. What proportion of the time was the simulation as big or bigger? (In statistics this is called the \(p\)-value.) (Choose an interval.)
    1. If the \(p\)-value is less than 0.05, then the difference is significant. Otherwise, the difference is not significant. Is the difference significant?
    1. So, then, can we conclude that Dante is significantly better than Yara at archery?


    Solution

    • \(\bar{x}_1= 229.583\)
    • \(\bar{x}_2= 211.875\)
    • \(|\bar{x}_2-\bar{x}_1| = 17.708\)
    • Your number of sim diffs less than the obs diff “should” be between 987 and 998. So you would pick interval \((950,1000)\).
    • Your number of sim diffs greater than or equal to the obs diff “should” be between 2 and 13. So you would pick interval \((0,50)\).
    • Your \(p\)-value should be between 0.002 and 0.013. So, you would pick interval \((0,0.05)\)
    • Yes. The difference is significant.
    • Yes. There is a detectible difference, and Dante scored higher on average

    Sample means (Questions 1 and 2)

    \[\bar{x}_1=\frac{224+236+235+230+236+216+210+237+239+234+226+232}{12} = 229.583\]

    \[\bar{x}_2=\frac{200+216+245+223+207+192+204+208}{8} = 211.875\]

    Observed absolute difference of means (Question 3)

    \[|\bar{x}_2-\bar{x}_1| ~=~ |211.875-229.583| ~=~ 17.708\]

    Simulation (Question 4)

    1. Use the two-list reshuffler with 1000 rows. You’ll notice that Dante had 12 scores and Yara had 8 scores. You should picture each score written on a card in a deck of 20 cards. You then shuffle the cards and deal them back into two piles with 12 cards and 8 cards.

    2. Copy all the data into a new spreadsheet. Click on A1 and paste (ctrl+v) to use A2:V1001 to hold the data. (The first row with be a header with labels.)

    3. With each repetition (row) you should find an absolute difference of means. You might need to add some more columns to the right of the data to follow these instructions.

      • An easy way to add a bunch of columns: press ctrl+a, maybe twice, to highlight all rows and columns; click Insert>Columns>Columns to the right.

      In cell X2, calculate the absolute difference of means with =ABS(AVERAGE(A2:L2)-AVERAGE(N2:V2)). Hit Enter. Select X2. Hit ctrl+shift+down and ctrl+enter to copy the formula down the whole column. You now have the 1000 simulated differences.

    4. Personally, I would now sort the sheet by column X.

    5. Now, we want to compare each (absolute) difference to the observed difference of 17.708. To do that, in cell Y2 write =IF(X2 < 17.708, 1, 0). Hit Enter. Click back on cell Y2; type ctrl+shift+down and ctrl+enter to copy the formula down.

    6. Count how many 1s (trues) there are. In cell Z2, write =SUM(Y:Y) to count up the ones.

      • I should mention that you could have just used =COUNTIF(X:X,"<17.708").

      You have your answer to question 4. I’ve made a visualization of my simulation. Your simulated data will be different but similar.


      For these instructions, we will say the answer to question 4 is 997.

    Question 5

    To get the answer to question 5, give the absolute difference between your answer to question 4 and 1000. So, for my simulation: \(1000-997=3\).

    Question 6

    Divide your answer to question 5 by 1000. So, for my simulation: \[p\mathrm{-value} = 0.003\]

    Question 7

    Because \(p-\mathrm{value} < 0.05\), we say the difference is significant.

    Question 8

    Because the difference is significant, and we know Dante scored higher, we can say Dante is a more skilled archer than Yara.
