  1. Question

    Consider the quadratic function, \(f(x)\), written in standard form:

    \[f(x)~=~{81}x^{2}+{16}\]

    The function \(f(x)\) can be rewritten as a product of two linear factors. Which of the following shows \(f(x)\) in factored form?


    1. \(f(x)~=~({9}x-{4i})\cdot({9}x-{4i})\)
    2. \(f(x)~=~({9}x-{4})\cdot({9}x-{4})\)
    3. \(f(x)~=~({9}x+{4i})\cdot({9}x+{4i})\)
    4. \(f(x)~=~({9}x+{4})\cdot({9}x+{4})\)
    5. \(f(x)~=~({9}x-{4i})\cdot({9}x+{4})\)
    6. \(f(x)~=~({9}x-{4})\cdot({9}x+{4})\)
    7. \(f(x)~=~({9}x-{4i})\cdot({9}x-{4})\)
    8. \(f(x)~=~({9}x-{4i})\cdot({9}x+{4i})\)
    9. \(f(x)~=~({9}x+{4i})\cdot({9}x+{4})\)
    10. \(f(x)~=~({9}x-{4})\cdot({9}x+{4i})\)

    Solution

    The correct answer is \(f(x)=({9}x-{4i})\cdot({9}x+{4i})\).

    We can show the correct answer is correct by using multiplication.

    * \(9x\) \(4i\)
    \(9x\) \(81x^2\) \(36i\)
    \(-4i\) \(-36i\) \(-16i^2\)

    \[({9}x-{4i})\cdot({9}x+{4i}) ~\equiv~ 81x^2+36ix-36ix-16i^2\]

    Remember, \(i^2=-1\).

    \[({9}x-{4i})\cdot({9}x+{4i}) ~\equiv~ 81x^2+36ix-36ix-16(-1)\]

    Eliminate opposite terms.

    \[({9}x-{4i})\cdot({9}x+{4i}) ~\equiv~ 81x^2+16\]

    This is a special case called a sum of squares. People usually memorize this as its own rule.

    \[(p^2x^2+q^2) ~\equiv~ (px-qi)(px+qi)\]

    or

    \[(Mx^2+N) ~\equiv~ (\sqrt{M}\,x-\sqrt{N}\,i)(\sqrt{M}\,x+\sqrt{N}\,i)\]

    You could also use the quadratic formula. We know that factors of \(f(x)\) are related to the solutions to the equation \(f(x)=0\). We also know that we can solve \(ax^2+bx+c=0\) for any values of \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) by using the quadratric formula.

    \[\text{If } ax^2+bx+c=0\] \[\text{then } x=\frac{-b\pm\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}\]

    First, solve \({81}x^{2}+{16}=0\) using quadratic formula. In this case, \(a=81\) and \(b=0\) and \(c=16\).

    \[x~=~\frac{-0\pm\sqrt{0^2-4(81)(16)}}{2(81)}\]

    Simplify by getting rid of the 0 terms.

    \[x~=~\frac{\pm\sqrt{-4(81)(16)}}{2(81)}\]

    The factor of 4 under the radical is a perfect square. \(4=2^2\), so \(\sqrt{4}=2\). Also, \(\sqrt{jk}\equiv\sqrt{j}\sqrt{k}\). You can know this based on exponent rules. Remember, \(\sqrt{t}\equiv t^{1/2}\). In short, you can “pull out the 4 as a 2”.

    \[x~=~\frac{\pm2\sqrt{-(81)(16)}}{2(81)}\]

    There is a common factor of 2 in numerator and denominator.

    \[x~=~\frac{\pm\sqrt{-(81)(16)}}{81}\]

    Here’s a fun fact: \(\frac{\sqrt{v}}{v} \equiv \frac{1}{\sqrt{v}}\). Use that property.

    \[x~=~\frac{\pm\sqrt{-16}}{\sqrt{81}}\]

    Both 16 and 81 are perfect squares.

    \[x~=~\frac{\pm4i}{9}\]

    You can get the factors (from those solutions) by getting the right side to equal 0.

    • Multiply both sides by 9.

    \[9x~=~\pm4i\]

    • Subtract \(\pm4i\) from both sides.

    \[9x\pm4i=0\]

    So,

    \[f(x)=({9}x-{4i})\cdot({9}x+{4i})\]

    Technically, this analysis just shows \(f(x)=A({9}x-{4i})\cdot({9}x+{4i})\), but we know \(A=1\) based on the answer choices.

    4. \(f(x)~=~({8}x+{9})\cdot({8}x+{9i})\)
    5. \(f(x)~=~({8}x+{9})\cdot({8}x+{9})\)
    6. \(f(x)~=~({8}x-{9i})\cdot({8}x+{9})\)
    7. \(f(x)~=~({8}x-{9})\cdot({8}x-{9})\)
    8. \(f(x)~=~({8}x-{9i})\cdot({8}x-{9})\)
    9. \(f(x)~=~({8}x-{9i})\cdot({8}x-{9i})\)
    10. \(f(x)~=~({8}x-{9})\cdot({8}x+{9})\)

    Solution

    The correct answer is \(f(x)=({8}x-{9i})\cdot({8}x+{9i})\).

    We can show the correct answer is correct by using multiplication.

    * \(8x\) \(9i\)
    \(8x\) \(64x^2\) \(72i\)
    \(-9i\) \(-72i\) \(-81i^2\)

    \[({8}x-{9i})\cdot({8}x+{9i}) ~\equiv~ 64x^2+72ix-72ix-81i^2\]

    Remember, \(i^2=-1\).

    \[({8}x-{9i})\cdot({8}x+{9i}) ~\equiv~ 64x^2+72ix-72ix-81(-1)\]

    Eliminate opposite terms.

    \[({8}x-{9i})\cdot({8}x+{9i}) ~\equiv~ 64x^2+81\]

    This is a special case called a sum of squares. People usually memorize this as its own rule.

    \[(p^2x^2+q^2) ~\equiv~ (px-qi)(px+qi)\]

    or

    \[(Mx^2+N) ~\equiv~ (\sqrt{M}\,x-\sqrt{N}\,i)(\sqrt{M}\,x+\sqrt{N}\,i)\]

    You could also use the quadratic formula. We know that factors of \(f(x)\) are related to the solutions to the equation \(f(x)=0\). We also know that we can solve \(ax^2+bx+c=0\) for any values of \(a\), \(b\), and \(c\) by using the quadratric formula.

    \[\text{If } ax^2+bx+c=0\] \[\text{then } x=\frac{-b\pm\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}\]

    First, solve \({64}x^{2}+{81}=0\) using quadratic formula. In this case, \(a=64\) and \(b=0\) and \(c=81\).

    \[x~=~\frac{-0\pm\sqrt{0^2-4(64)(81)}}{2(64)}\]

    Simplify by getting rid of the 0 terms.

    \[x~=~\frac{\pm\sqrt{-4(64)(81)}}{2(64)}\]

    The factor of 4 under the radical is a perfect square. \(4=2^2\), so \(\sqrt{4}=2\). Also, \(\sqrt{jk}\equiv\sqrt{j}\sqrt{k}\). You can know this based on exponent rules. Remember, \(\sqrt{t}\equiv t^{1/2}\). In short, you can “pull out the 4 as a 2”.

    \[x~=~\frac{\pm2\sqrt{-(64)(81)}}{2(64)}\]

    There is a common factor of 2 in numerator and denominator.

    \[x~=~\frac{\pm\sqrt{-(64)(81)}}{64}\]

    Here’s a fun fact: \(\frac{\sqrt{v}}{v} \equiv \frac{1}{\sqrt{v}}\). Use that property.

    \[x~=~\frac{\pm\sqrt{-81}}{\sqrt{64}}\]

    Both 81 and 64 are perfect squares.

    \[x~=~\frac{\pm9i}{8}\]

    You can get the factors (from those solutions) by getting the right side to equal 0.

    • Multiply both sides by 8.

    \[8x~=~\pm9i\]

    • Subtract \(\pm9i\) from both sides.

    \[8x\pm9i=0\]

    So,

    \[f(x)=({8}x-{9i})\cdot({8}x+{9i})\]

    Technically, this analysis just shows \(f(x)=A({8}x-{9i})\cdot({8}x+{9i})\), but we know \(A=1\) based on the answer choices.
