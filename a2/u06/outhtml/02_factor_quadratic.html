  1. Question

    The quadratic polynomial \(p(x)\) is defined below.

    \[p(x)~=~x^{2}-{13}x+{36}\]

    Which two linear polynomials are factors of \(p(x)\)?


    1. \(x-{12}\)
    2. \(x-{11}\)
    3. \(x-{10}\)
    4. \(x-{9}\)
    5. \(x-{8}\)
    6. \(x-{7}\)
    7. \(x-{6}\)
    8. \(x-{5}\)
    9. \(x-{4}\)
    10. \(x-{3}\)
    11. \(x-{2}\)
    12. \(x-{1}\)
    13. \(x+{1}\)
    14. \(x+{2}\)
    15. \(x+{3}\)
    16. \(x+{4}\)
    17. \(x+{5}\)
    18. \(x+{6}\)
    19. \(x+{7}\)
    20. \(x+{8}\)
    21. \(x+{9}\)
    22. \(x+{10}\)
    23. \(x+{11}\)
    24. \(x+{12}\)

    Solution

    You can get this by guessing and checking. Eventually you might guess the following:

    \[p(x) ~=~ (x-{9})\cdot(x-{4})\]

    You can check by using polynomial multiplication (in this case you can use the FOIL method).

    * \(x\) \(-9\)
    \(x\) \(x^2\) \({-9}x\)
    \(-4\) \({-4}x\) \(36\)

    \[p(x) ~=~ x^{2}-{9}x-{4}x+{36}\]

    \[p(x) ~=~ x^{2}-{13}x+{36}\]

    After you’ve done this a few hundred times, you’ll notice that you wanted two numbers that added to give -13 and multiplied to give 36. So, you start thinking of factor pairs of 36 until you find a pair that sums to -13. You will see that below I’ve listed all factor pairs of 36, along with the corresponding polynomials in factored and expanded forms.

    Factor pairs of 36 Factored polynomial Expanded polynomial
    1 and 36 \((x+{1})\cdot (x+{36})\) \(x^{2}+{37}x+{36}\)
    -1 and -36 \((x-{1})\cdot (x-{36})\) \(x^{2}-{37}x+{36}\)
    2 and 18 \((x+{2})\cdot (x+{18})\) \(x^{2}+{20}x+{36}\)
    -2 and -18 \((x-{2})\cdot (x-{18})\) \(x^{2}-{20}x+{36}\)
    3 and 12 \((x+{3})\cdot (x+{12})\) \(x^{2}+{15}x+{36}\)
    -3 and -12 \((x-{3})\cdot (x-{12})\) \(x^{2}-{15}x+{36}\)
    4 and 9 \((x+{4})\cdot (x+{9})\) \(x^{2}+{13}x+{36}\)
    -4 and -9 \((x-{4})\cdot (x-{9})\) \(x^{2}-{13}x+{36}\)
    6 and 6 \((x+{6})\cdot (x+{6})\) \(x^{2}+{12}x+{36}\)
    -6 and -6 \((x-{6})\cdot (x-{6})\) \(x^{2}-{12}x+{36}\)
