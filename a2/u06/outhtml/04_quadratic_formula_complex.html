  1. Question

    The quadratic formula says if \(ax^2+bx+c=0\) then \(x=\frac{-b\pm\sqrt{b^2-4ac}}{2a}\).

    The radicand is called the discriminant. If the discriminant is negative, or, as an algebraic inequality, if \(b^2-4ac<0\), then the solutions are complex. When a quadratic equation has complex solutions, those solutions are always complex conjugates.

    Use the quadratic formula to solve

    \[x^2-2x+37=0\]

    Remember: \(i=\sqrt{-1}\).


    1. 1+6i
    2. -6+i
    3. -1-6i
    4. 6-i
    5. -6-i
    6. -1+6i
    7. 1-6i
    8. 6+i

    Solution

    Plug numbers into the quadratic formula.

    \[x = \frac{-(-2)\pm\sqrt{(-2)^2-4(1)(37)}}{2(1)} \]

    Simplify.

    \[x = \frac{2 \pm \sqrt{-144}}{2}\]

    \[x = \frac{2 \pm 12i}{2}\]

    \[x = 1+6i ~~~~~~~~\text{OR}~~~~~~~~ x=1-6i\]

    I should point out this relates to factoring because we could rewrite the original equation in factored form.

    \[(x-(1+6i))\cdot(x-(1-6i))~=~0\]

    We need to practice multiplying complex numbers before you’d be able to check this though.
