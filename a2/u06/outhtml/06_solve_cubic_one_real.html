    Solve:

    \[x^3+13x^2+39x-53=0\]

    • The real solution:
    • The first complex solution: -\(i\)
    • The other complex solution: +\(i\)

    Hint: the real solution is an integer between -6 and 6. Also the rational root theorem places additional constraints on possible real solutions. Descarte’s rule of signs can also help eliminate possibilities.



    Solution

    • Guess that \((x-1)\) is a factor:
      1 13 39 -53
      1 1 14 53
      1 14 53 0
      Notice, the remainder is 0, so the guess was good! So, we rephrase the original equation into a semi-factored form.\[(x-1)(x^2+14x+53)=0\] I would recommend using quadratic formula to determine the complex roots.\[a=1\] \[b=14\] \[c=53\]

    Plug numbers into the quadratic formula.

    \[x = \frac{-(14)\pm\sqrt{(14)^2-4(1)(53)}}{2(1)} \]

    Simplify.

    \[x = \frac{-14 \pm \sqrt{-16}}{2}\]

    \[x = \frac{-14 \pm 4i}{2}\]

    \[x = -7+2i ~~~~~~~~\text{ OR }~~~~~~~~ x=-7-2i\]

    We could rewrite the original equation in fully factored form.

    \[\Big(x-(1)\Big)\cdot\Big(x-(-7-2i)\Big)\cdot\Big(x-(-7+2i)\Big)~~=~~0\]

    The solutions are: \[x = 1\] \[x = -7-2i\] \[x = -7+2i\]
