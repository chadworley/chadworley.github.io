  1. Question

    A polynomial, \(p(x)\), is given in factored form. \[p(x)~=~-(x+6)\cdot (x+3)^{2}\]

    Which of the following graphs matches \(y=p(x)\)?



    Solution

    First, consider the end behavior. If you expanded the polynomial into standard form, the highest-degree term would be \(-x^{3}\).

    \[p(x)~=~-x^{3}+\ldots\]

    The highest degree term has a negative coefficient and an odd degree, so the end behavior is “up,down”. Or, more formally, \(\lim\limits_{x\to-\infty} p(x) ~=~ \infty\) and \(\lim\limits_{x\to\infty} p(x) ~=~ -\infty\).

    Next, we consider the roots. From the factored form, \(p(x)~=~-(x+6)\cdot (x+3)^{2}\), it is easy to see (see zero-product property) that \(x=-6\) or \(x=-3\) makes the polynomial output 0.

    \[p(-6)~=~-(-6+6)\cdot (-6+3)^{2}~=~0\] \[p(-3)~=~-(-3+6)\cdot (-3+3)^{2}~=~0\]

    We predict that at \(x=-3\) there is a double root (multiplicity of 2) because the corresponding factor is squared. Graphically, we expect the curve to “bounce” off the \(x\) axis at \(x=-3\). At the other root, we expect the curve to cut through the \(x\) axis.

    Anyway, based on this analysis, we can produce a sign graph of the polynomial.

    \(x\) interval or value sign analysis of \(p(x)~=~-(x+6)\cdot (x+3)^{2}\) sign
    \((-\infty,-6)\) \(-(\mathrm{neg})\cdot (\mathrm{neg})^{2}\) \(1\)
    \(-6\) \(-(\mathrm{zero})\cdot (\mathrm{neg})^{2}\) \(0\)
    \((-6,-3)\) \(-(\mathrm{pos})\cdot (\mathrm{neg})^{2}\) \(-1\)
    \(-3\) \(-(\mathrm{pos})\cdot (\mathrm{zero})^{2}\) \(0\)
    \((-3,\infty)\) \(-(\mathrm{pos})\cdot (\mathrm{pos})^{2}\) \(-1\)


    And only one option matches.
