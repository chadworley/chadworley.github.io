  1. Question

    Quadratic polynomial \(p\) is defined below in standard form. \[p(x)~=~x^{2}+{4}x-{1}\]

    By completing the square, you can rewrite the quadratic polynomial in vertex form:

    \[p(x) = a(x-h)^2+k\]

    where \((h,k)\) is the vertex of the parabola (and \(a\) is the same as the quadratic coefficient, which equals 1 in this problem.)

    Determine the coordinates of the vertex of quadratic polynomial \(p\).

    • \(h=\)
    • \(k=\)


    Solution

    Helpful video: Khan academy

    Here is a list of some perfect squares:

    \[\begin{align*} (x-5)^2 ~&=~ x^2-10x+25 \\ (x-4)^2 ~&=~ x^2-8x+16 \\ (x-3)^2 ~&=~ x^2-6x+9 \\ (x-2)^2 ~&=~ x^2-4x+4 \\ (x-1)^2 ~&=~ x^2-2x+1 \\ (x+1)^2 ~&=~ x^2+2x+1 \\ (x+2)^2 ~&=~ x^2+4x+4 \\ (x+3)^2 ~&=~ x^2+6x+9 \\ (x+4)^2 ~&=~ x^2+8x+16 \\ (x+5)^2 ~&=~ x^2+10x+25 \end{align*}\]

    Notice that every perfect square in standard form has the form \((x+j)^2~=~x^2+2jx+j^2\).

    We are given \(p(x)~=~x^{2}+{4}x-{1}\). We can show that since our quadratic coefficient is 1 and our linear coefficient is 4, we just need to square half of 4 to “complete the square”.

    \[\left(\frac{4}{2}\right)^2 ~=~ \left(2\right)^2 ~=~ 4\]

    We then add and subtract this amount to the polynomial…

    \[p(x) ~=~ x^{2}+{4}x+{4}-{1}-{4}\]

    Notice the first three terms are a perfect square. Rewrite them in factored form.

    \[p(x) ~=~ \left(x+{2}\right)^2-{1}-{4}\]

    Combine the constants.

    \[p(x) ~=~ \left(x+{2}\right)^2-{5}\]

    And so:

    \[h = -2\] \[k = -5\]
