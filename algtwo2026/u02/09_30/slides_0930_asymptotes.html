Asymptotes in parent functions

  • An asymptote of a curve is a straight line such that the distance between the curve and the line approaches zero as one or both of the \(x\) or \(y\) coordinates tends to infinity.
  • Examples:
    • Exponential parent function, \(y=2^x\), has a horizontal asymptote \(y=0\), which is approached as \(x\to -\infty\).

  • Logarithmic parent function, \(y=\log_2(x)\), has a vertical asymptote \(x=0\), which is approached as \(y\to -\infty\).

  • Reciprocal parent function, \(y=\frac{1}{x}\), has two asymptotes at \(y=0\) and \(x=0\).
  • Tangent parent function, \(y=\tan(x)\), has vertical asymptotes at \(x=\pi/2+n\pi\) for any integer \(n\).

Shifting with asymptotes

Asymptotes shift when the function shifts.

  • Examples:
    • Consider \(y=2^{x-3}+4\). Notice the horizontal asymptote shifts up to \(y=4\).

  • Consider \(y=\log_2(x-3)+4\). Notice the vertical asymptote shifts right to \(x=3\).

  • Consider \(y=\frac{1}{x-3}+4\).
