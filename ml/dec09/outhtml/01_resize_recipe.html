  1. Question

    A recipe calls for 15 ounces of flour and 10 ounces of sugar. You resize the recipe to use 12 ounces of flour and \(x\) ounces of sugar. Find \(x\).


    Solution

    There are a few different approaches to this problem.

    Strategy 1: equivalent fractions

    I’d first simplify the ratio by recognizing that both \(15\) and \(10\) are multiples of 5.

    \[\frac{15}{10} ~=~ \frac{15\div 5}{10\div 5} ~=~ \frac{3}{2}\]

    Then, I’d notice that 12 is a multiple of 3, so I’d scale up the recipe.

    \[\frac{3}{2} ~=~ \frac{3\cdot 4}{2\cdot 4} ~=~ \frac{12}{8}\]

    And, thus, the resized recipe calls for 8 ounces of sugar.

    Strategy 2: simplify the scale factor.

    The scale factor equals the new amount of flour divided by the old amount of flour.

    \[k ~=~ \frac{12}{15}\]

    Simplify the scale factor by recognizing both 12 and 15 are multiples of 3.

    \[k ~=~ \frac{12\div 3}{15 \div 3} ~=~ \frac{4}{5} \]

    Then, multiply the old amount of sugar by the scale factor.

    \[10\cdot \frac{4}{5} ~=~ \frac{10\cdot 4}{5} ~=~ \frac{10}{5}\cdot 4 ~=~ 2\cdot 4 ~=~ 8\]

    And, thus, the resized recipe calls for 8 ounces of sugar.
