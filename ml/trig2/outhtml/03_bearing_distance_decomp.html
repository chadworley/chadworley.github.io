  1. Question

    In navigation, direction is often given as a bearing, which is in degrees clockwise from north. If the north used as reference is the true geographical north (as opposed to magnetic north) then the bearing is a true bearing.

    An adventurer walks 329 kilometers at a true bearing of 152Â°. Assuming that a flat-earth approximation is valid for this short distance (and the bearing can be maintained by walking in a straight line because the adventurer is not near a pole), find the distance traveled N/S and E/W.



    Solution

    Draw a sketch. Remember, bearings are given as degrees CLOCKWISE from NORTH. This is different from how mathematicians typically indicate direction as radians counterclockwise from east.


    In this system, to get the âverticalâ component, you can multiply the distance by the cosine of the angle (in DEGREE MODE). To get the âhorizontalâ component, multiply the distance by the sine of the angle. Notice, the roles of sine and cosine are switched around.

    \[329\cdot \cos(152^{\circ}) = -290.4897581\] \[329\cdot \sin(152^{\circ}) = 154.4561442\]

    So, approximately 290 kilometers South and approximately 154 kilometers East.
