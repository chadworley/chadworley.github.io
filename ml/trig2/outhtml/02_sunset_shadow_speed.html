02_sunset_shadow_speed


  1. Question

    At sunset, the sun’s rays are parallel to the local flat ground. A rocket is launched at an angle of elevation of 32.3° above the horizon at a speed of 3120 meters per second. Its shadow is projected onto a tall vertical cliff to the east.


    How fast, in meters per second, is the shadow moving up the cliff? The tolerance is \(\pm 10\) m/s.


    Solution

    We recognize that velocity is a vector. In this scenario, we can think of the vector as a 2D vector given in polar form.

    Using basic right-triangle trigonometry, you can find the horizontal component of the velocity.

    Draw a vector with magnitude (length) of 3120 m/s and angle of 32.3 degrees. We can indicate the magnitude with double vertical lines on either side of the vector’s name. We can also indicate magnitude by leaving off the arrow.

    \[||\vec{v}||~=~ v ~=~3120~\mathrm{\frac{m}{s}}\]

    We want to decompose the vector into its horizontal component (\(\vec{v}_x\)) and its vertical component (\(\vec{v}_y\)). The vertical component’s magnitude (denoted as \(||\vec{v}_y||\), or more simply as \(v_y\)) is the speed of the shadow.


    We can set up the sine equation.

    \[\sin(32.3^{\circ})~=~\frac{v_y}{3120}\]

    Solve for \(v_y\).

    \[v_y~=~3120\cdot\sin(32.3^{\circ})\]

    Evaluate with a calculator. Remember, we are in DEGREE MODE!

    \[v_y~=~1667.1793301\] \[v_y~\approx~1670\]
