  1. Question

    At noon, on an equinox, at the equator, the sun is directly overhead. Over land with minimal elevation change, we can approximate the local ground as flat.

    A rocket is launched at an angle of elevation of 32.3Â° above the horizon at a speed of 3120 meters per second.


    How fast, in meters per second, is the shadow moving across the flat ground? The tolerance is \(\pm 10\) m/s.


    Solution

    We recognize that velocity is a vector. In this scenario, we can think of the vector as a 2D vector given in polar form.

    Using basic right-triangle trigonometry, you can find the horizontal component of the velocity.

    Draw a vector with magnitude (length) of 3120 m/s and angle of 32.3 degrees. We can indicate the magnitude with double vertical lines on either side of the vectorâs name. We can also indicate magnitude by leaving off the arrow.

    \[||\vec{v}||~=~ v ~=~3120~\mathrm{\frac{m}{s}}\]

    We want to decompose the vector into its horizontal component (\(\vec{v}_x\)) and its vertical component (\(\vec{v}_y\)). The horizontal componentâs magnitude (denoted as \(||\vec{v}_x||\), or more simply as \(v_x\)) is the speed of the shadow.


    We can set up the cosine equation.

    \[\cos(32.3^{\circ})~=~\frac{v_x}{3120}\]

    Solve for \(v_x\).

    \[v_x~=~3120\cdot\cos(32.3^{\circ})\]

    Evaluate with a calculator. Remember, we are in DEGREE MODE!

    \[v_x~=~2637.2169197\] \[v_x~\approx~2640\]
