  1. Question

    A lottery offers the following payout scheme:

    profit chance
    -9 0.4
    2 0.3
    6 0.2
    8 0.1

    To analyze this discrete probability distribution, you can use this link:

    https://chadworley.github.io/cltsim.html?poss=-9*2*6*8&wght=0.4*0.3*0.2*0.1

    A friend claims, “I only played that lottery \(n=25\) times, and I’ve won \(\sum x = 86\) dollars.” You wish to judge whether your friend is making an outlandish claim. To do this, you calculate a \(z\) score.

    \[z = \frac{\sum x - n\mu}{\sigma\sqrt{n}}\]

    A typical \(z\) score is between \(-2\) and \(2\) (this happens 95% of the time). A \(z\) score less than \(-3\) or more than \(3\) is quite unlikely (less than 0.3% of the time).

    Is your friend’s claim typical or outlandish? To answer this, calculate a \(z\) score accurate to the hundredths place.

    \[z=\,?\]


    Solution

    Use the link to determine the population mean \(\mu=-1\) and population standard deviation \(\sigma = 6.7970582\).

    \[z = \frac{\sum x - n\mu}{\sigma\sqrt{n}}\]

    \[z = \frac{(86)-(25)(-1)}{(6.7970582)\sqrt{25}} \]

    \[z = \frac{(86)-(-25)}{(33.9852909)} \]

    \[z = 3.27 \]


  2. Question

    A lottery offers the following payout scheme:

    profit chance
    -9 0.3
    0 0.1
    1 0.4
    6 0.2

    Calculate \(\mu\), the population mean.

    \[\mu = \sum x_i \cdot p_i\]

    This formula tells you to multiply each profit by its chance, and then add up all those products.


    Solution

    \[\mu = (-9)(0.3)+(0)(0.1)+(1)(0.4)+(6)(0.2)\]

    \[\mu = -2.7 + 0 + 0.4 + 1.2 \]

    \[\mu = -1.1 \]

    You could also use the app:

    https://chadworley.github.io/cltsim.html?poss=-9*0*1*6&wght=0.3*0.1*0.4*0.2
