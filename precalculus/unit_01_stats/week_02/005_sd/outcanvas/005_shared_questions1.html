  1. Question

    A lottery machine (population) will produce random numbers with replacement. It has 5 balls, with the following numbers:

    \(x_i\)
    57
    65
    53
    61
    59


    Please calculate the population standard deviation. To do this, first calculate the population mean.

    \[\mu = \frac{\sum\limits_{i=1}^n x_i}{n}\]

    Calculate the population variance by averaging the squared deviations. \[\text{variance} = \sigma^2 = \frac{\sum\limits_{i=1}^n (x_i-\mu)^2}{n} \]

    Determine the population standard deviation by taking the square root of the population variance. \[\sigma = \sqrt{\sigma^2} \]


    Solution

    Find the mean. \[\mu = \frac{57+65+53+61+59}{5} = 59 \]

    Find the variance.

    \[\sigma^2 = \frac{(57-59)^2+(65-59)^2+(53-59)^2+(61-59)^2+(59-59)^2}{5} \]

    \[\sigma^2 = \frac{(-2)^2+(6)^2+(-6)^2+(2)^2+(0)^2}{5}\]

    \[\sigma^2 = \frac{4+36+36+4+0}{5}\]

    \[\sigma^2 = 16\] Calculate the standard deviation. \[\sigma = \sqrt{16} = 4 \]

    A small sample was gathered. The measurements are below.

    \(x_i\)
    78
    60
    68
    65
    65
    66

    Please calculate the sample standard deviation. To do this, first calculate the sample mean.

    \[\bar{x} = \frac{\sum\limits_{i=1}^n x_i}{n}\]

    Calculate the sample variance by dividing the sum of the squared deviations by \(n-1\). This subtlety (of replacing the denominator with \(n-1\) instead of \(n\)) is called Bessel’s correction. This provides an unbiased estimation of the population’s variance when the population’s mean is also unknown. \[\text{sample variance (with Bessel correction)} = s^2 = \frac{\sum\limits_{i=1}^n (x_i-\bar{x})^2}{n-1} \]

    Determine the sample standard deviation by taking the square root of the sample variance. \[s = \sqrt{s^2} \]


    Solution

    Find the sample mean. \[\bar{x} = \frac{78+60+68+65+65+66}{6} = 67 \]

    Find the variance.

    \[s^2 = \frac{(78-67)^2+(60-67)^2+(68-67)^2+(65-67)^2+(65-67)^2+(66-67)^2}{5} \]

    \[s^2 = \frac{(11)^2+(-7)^2+(1)^2+(-2)^2+(-2)^2+(-1)^2}{5}\]

    \[s^2 = \frac{121+49+1+4+4+1}{5}\]

    \[s^2 = 36 \] Calculate the standard deviation. \[s = \sqrt{36} = 6 \]

  3. Question

    A lottery has the discrete probability distribution below.

    \(x_i\) \(p_i\)
    -6 0.4
    0 0.2
    1 0.1
    7 0.3

    Find the population standard deviation. To do this first calculate the population mean.

    \[\mu = \sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}p_ix_i\]

    Calculate the variance.

    \[\sigma^2 = \sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}p_i(x_i-\mu)^2\]

    Finally, take the square root of the variance to get the standard deviation.

    \[\sigma = \sqrt{\sigma^2}\]


    Solution

    Calculate the mean.

    \[\mu = (-6)(0.4)+(0)(0.2)+(1)(0.1)+(7)(0.3) \] \[\mu = -2.4+0+0.1+2.1 \] \[\mu = -0.2 \]

    Next, calculate the variance.

    \[\sigma^2 = (0.4)(-6-(-0.2))^2+(0.2)(0-(-0.2))^2+(0.1)(1-(-0.2))^2+(0.3)(7-(-0.2))^2 \]

    \[\sigma^2 = (0.4)(-5.8)^2+(0.2)(0.2)^2+(0.1)(1.2)^2+(0.3)(7.2)^2 \]

    \[\sigma^2 = 13.456+0.008+0.144+15.552 \]

    \[\sigma^2 = 29.16\] Get the standard deviation by taking the square root of the variance.

    \[\sigma = 5.4\]

  4. Question

    A large sample (\(n=10000\)) was gathered and visualized with a histogram.


    When a symmetric distribution is sampled thoroughly (\(n>100\)), you can use the following rules of thumb to estimate the mean and standard deviation from a histogram.

    Shape Estimated mean Estimated standard deviation
    Bell \(\frac{\min(x)+\max(x)}{2}\) \(\frac{\max(x)-\min(x)}{6}\)
    Uniform \(\frac{\min(x)+\max(x)}{2}\) \(\frac{\max(x)-\min(x)}{\sqrt{12}}\)
    Bimodal \(\frac{\min(x)+\max(x)}{2}\) \(\frac{\max(x)-\min(x)}{2}\)
    • Estimate the standard deviation of the sample. (Please use the given formula.)

    Solution

    video

    Notice the bell has the smallest standard deviation for a given range. This is because many measurements are near the mean, and just a few are near the edges.

    The bimodal has the largest standard deviation for a given range. This is because many measurements are near the edges, and just a few are near the mean.

    The uniform distribution has about equal numbers of measurements near the mean and near the edges, so it has a standard deviation between the two other shapes (for a given range).

    You’ll notice the bimodal estimate is probably the worst estimate. The estimate would be more accurate if none of the values were near the mean, and all the values were near the edge.

    It should be noted that many bell-shaped distributions can technically have an infinite range of measurements. You would ignore the “outliers” for this estimation (and not have a monstrously large sample size).

    • By using the formula, you’ll get an estimate of 1.6666667 for the standard deviation. The actual standard deviation is 1.6836613.

  5. Question

    Joe will play two lotteries, with the following profit distributions, mean, and standard deviations.

    Lottery 1

    \(x_i\) \(p_i\)
    -6 0.4
    0 0.1
    1 0.3
    7 0.2

    \[\mu_1 = -0.7\] \[\sigma_1 = 4.9\]

    Lottery 2

    \(x_i\) \(p_i\)
    -8 0.3
    0 0.4
    2 0.2
    5 0.1

    \[\mu_2 = -1.5\] \[\sigma_2 = 4.5\]

    Total of both lotteries

    There are many possible outcomes for the total of both lotteries. Each pairing of outcomes is a possibility, giving a profit (from the sum of the two profits) and a chance (from the product of the two chances).

    profit chance
    -14 0.12
    -6 0.16
    -4 0.08
    -1 0.04
    -8 0.03
    0 0.04
    2 0.02
    5 0.01
    -7 0.09
    1 0.12
    3 0.06
    6 0.03
    -1 0.06
    7 0.08
    9 0.04
    12 0.02

    Notice, some possible profits show up more than once; these could be collapsed by adding their probabilities. It would still be a pain to analyze by hand. Luckily, there are some very nice properties when considering the sum of random variables.

    \[\mu = \mu_1 + \mu_2\]

    \[\sigma^2 = (\sigma_1)^2+(\sigma_2)^2\]

    \[\sigma = \sqrt{(\sigma_1)^2+(\sigma_2)^2} \]

    Find the population standard deviation of the profit distribution when Joe plays both lotteries, arriving at a combined profit by adding the two random independent profits.


    Solution

    This looks much worse than it is. \[\sigma = \sqrt{(4.9)^2+(4.5)^2} = 6.652819 \]

    Notice, there is no simple formula like this for the average absolute deviation of a sum of random variables. This is the key reason to use standard deviation (and variance) as our measure of spread.
