  1. Question

    In a deck of strange cards, there are 349 cards. Each card has an image and a color.


    1. What is the probability a random card is a pig?
    2. What is the probability a random card is blue?
    3. What is the probability a random card is both a pig and yellow?
    4. What is the probability a random card is either a vase or red (or both)?
    5. What is the probability a random card is red given it is a vase?
    6. What is the probability a random card is a pig given it is red?
    7. Between a lamp and a vase, which is more likely to be a red?


    Solution

    video

    The key logical terms are “and”, “or”, and “given”. Notice that I am using “given” as a shorter version of “under the condition”.

    1. \(\mathbb{P}[\text{pig}]=\frac{142}{349}=0.41\)
    2. \(\mathbb{P}[\text{blue}]=\frac{118}{349}=0.34\)
    3. \(\mathbb{P}[\text{pig and yellow}]=\frac{36}{349}=0.1\)
    4. \(\mathbb{P}[\text{vase or red}]=\frac{96+76-15}{349}=0.45\)
    5. \(\mathbb{P}[\text{red given vase}]=\frac{15}{96}=0.16\)
    6. \(\mathbb{P}[\text{pig given red}]=\frac{45}{76}=0.59\)
    7. \(\mathbb{P}[\text{red given lamp}] = 0.14\) and \(\mathbb{P}[\text{red given vase}] = 0.16\), so a vase is more likely to be red than a lamp is.

  5. Question

    Cindy has two games today. Each game she will either win or lose. She has a 0.62 chance of winning the first game and a 0.35 chance of winning the second game.

    1. What is the chance Cindy wins both games?
    2. What is the chance Cindy loses both games?
    3. What is the chance Cindy wins once and loses once (in either order)?


    Solution

    video

    \[P(WW) = 0.62 \times 0.35 = 0.217 \] \[P(LL) = 0.38 \times 0.65 = 0.247 \] \[P(WL) = 0.62\times 0.65 = 0.403 \] \[P(LW) = 0.38\times 0.35 = 0.133\]

    • P(win,win) = 0.62 * 0.35 = 0.217
    • P(lose,lose) = (1-0.62) * (1-0.35) = 0.247
    • P(WL or LW) = 1-0.217-0.247 = 0.536

  7. Question

    Bob has a 0.63 chance of winning a game. If Bob wins, he has a 0.89 chance of being happy after the game. If Bob loses, he has a 0.31 chance of being happy after the game.

    After the game, you notice Bob is happy. What is the probability that Bob won his game? (Do not answer as a percentage; answer as a decimal.)


    Solution

    (ANS=0.8301747)

    video

    Use the definition of conditional probability. \[P(\text{win given happy}) = \frac{P(\text{win and happy})}{P(\text{happy})}\] We can first determine all the joint probabilities. \[P(A\text{ and }B) = P(A)\cdot P(B\text{ given } A) \] \[\begin{aligned} P(\text{win and happy}) &= 0.63\times0.89 = 0.5607\\ P(\text{win and sad}) &= 0.63\times(1-0.89) = 0.0693\\ P(\text{lose and happy}) &= (1-0.63)\times0.31 = 0.1147\\ P(\text{lose and sad}) &= (1-0.63)\times(1-0.31) = 0.2553\\ \end{aligned}\]

    Notice there are two disjoint ways Bob could be happy. \[\begin{aligned} P(\text{happy}) &= P\left(\text{[win and happy] OR [lose and happy]}\right) \\ &= P(\text{win and happy}) + P(\text{lose and happy}) \\ &= 0.5607+0.1147 \\ &= 0.6754 \end{aligned}\] So, back to the conditional probability. \[P(\text{win given happy}) = \frac{0.5607}{0.6754} = 0.83\]
