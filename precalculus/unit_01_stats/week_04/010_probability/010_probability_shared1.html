  1. Question

    In a deck of strange cards, there are 499 cards. Each card has an image and a color.


    (Answers can be rounded to nearest hundredth.)

    1. What is the probability a random card is both a bike and gray?
    2. What is the probability a random card is white?
    3. What is the probability a random card is either a gem or pink (or both)?
    4. Between a gem and a jar, which is more likely to be a pink?
    5. What is the probability a random card is a kite?
    6. What is the probability a random card is a gem given it is pink?
    7. What is the probability a random card is gray given it is a bike?


    Solution

    video

    The key logical terms are “and”, “or”, and “given”. Notice that I am using “given” as a shorter version of “under the condition”.

    [1] “\(\\mathbb{P}[\\text{bike and gray}]=\\frac{35}{499}=0.07\)”
    [2] “\(\\mathbb{P}[\\text{white}]=\\frac{138}{499}=0.28\)”
    [3] “\(\\mathbb{P}[\\text{gem or pink}]=\\frac{131+168-48}{499}=0.5\)”
    [4] “\(\\mathbb{P}[\\text{pink given gem}] = 0.37\) and \(\\mathbb{P}[\\text{pink given jar}] = 0.25\), so a gem is more likely to be pink than a jar is.” [5] “.”
    [6] “\(\\mathbb{P}[\\text{kite}]=\\frac{95}{499}=0.19\)”
    [7] “\(\\mathbb{P}[\\text{gem given pink}]=\\frac{48}{168}=0.29\)”
    [8] “\(\\mathbb{P}[\\text{gray given bike}]=\\frac{35}{74}=0.47\)”

  3. Question

    Cindy has two games today. Each game she will either win or lose. She has a 0.77 chance of winning the first game and a 0.25 chance of winning the second game.

    1. What is the chance Cindy wins both games?
    2. What is the chance Cindy loses both games?
    3. What is the chance Cindy wins once and loses once (in either order)?


    Solution

    video

    \[P(WW) = 0.77 \times 0.25 = 0.1925 \] \[P(LL) = 0.23 \times 0.75 = 0.1725 \] \[P(WL) = 0.77\times 0.75 = 0.5775 \] \[P(LW) = 0.23\times 0.25 = 0.0575\]

    • P(win,win) = 0.77 * 0.25 = 0.1925
    • P(lose,lose) = (1-0.77) * (1-0.25) = 0.1725
    • P(WL or LW) = 1-0.1925-0.1725 = 0.635

    1. What is the chance Cindy wins both games?

  5. Question

    Bob has a 0.48 chance of winning a game. If Bob wins, he has a 0.63 chance of being happy after the game. If Bob loses, he has a 0.44 chance of being happy after the game.

    After the game, you notice Bob is happy. What is the probability that Bob won his game? (Do not answer as a percentage; answer as a decimal.)


    Solution

    (ANS=0.5692771)

    video

    Use the definition of conditional probability. \[P(\text{win given happy}) = \frac{P(\text{win and happy})}{P(\text{happy})}\] We can first determine all the joint probabilities. \[P(A\text{ and }B) = P(A)\cdot P(B\text{ given } A) \] \[\begin{aligned} P(\text{win and happy}) &= 0.48\times0.63 = 0.3024\\ P(\text{win and sad}) &= 0.48\times(1-0.63) = 0.1776\\ P(\text{lose and happy}) &= (1-0.48)\times0.44 = 0.2288\\ P(\text{lose and sad}) &= (1-0.48)\times(1-0.44) = 0.2912\\ \end{aligned}\]

    Notice there are two disjoint ways Bob could be happy. \[\begin{aligned} P(\text{happy}) &= P\left(\text{[win and happy] OR [lose and happy]}\right) \\ &= P(\text{win and happy}) + P(\text{lose and happy}) \\ &= 0.3024+0.2288 \\ &= 0.5312 \end{aligned}\] So, back to the conditional probability. \[P(\text{win given happy}) = \frac{0.3024}{0.5312} = 0.569\]
