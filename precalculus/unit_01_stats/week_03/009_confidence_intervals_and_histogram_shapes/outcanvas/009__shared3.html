  2. Question

    A scientist has weighed 104 specimens of a newly discovered organism. Those weights have a sample mean equaling \(\bar{x} =97.8\) grams and a sample standard deviation equaling \(s=29.7\) grams. The scientist hopes to construct a 95% confidence interval of the organism’s population mean (\(\mu\)).

    The scientist will later consult a statistician for a more precise method, but for now she will use a quick method to estimate the 95% confidence interval: \[\text{CI boundaries} = \bar{x}\pm \frac{2s}{\sqrt{n}} \]

    The lower boundary is .

    The upper boundary is .



    Solution

    Plug the numbers into the expressions.

    • Lower bound = \(97.8-2(29.7)/\sqrt{104} ~=~ 92\)
    • Upper bound = \(97.8+2(29.7)/\sqrt{104} ~=~ 103.6\)

    Using interval notation, the 95% confidence interval is [92, 103.6].

  4. Question

    A pollster is trying to determine the proportion of likely voters who are still undecided (note: in Statistics, the definition of “proportion” is distinct from other definitions; it refers to a quotient of counts, part over whole, giving a number between 0 and 1.) They randomly select 243 likely voters, and in that sample 83.1% are still undecided. The pollster wishes to calculate the boundaries of a confidence interval of the proportion.

    Proportions are a special case: an average of 0s (not undecided) and 1s (undecided). In this special case, the single parameter (sample proportion \(\hat{p}=0.831\)) determines the sample mean \(\bar{x}\) and the sample standard deviation \(s\).

    \[\bar{x} = \hat{p}\]

    \[s = \sqrt{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})} \]

    The approximate formula to calculate the boundaries of a confidence interval remains the same, except Bessel’s correction is not used to calculate \(s\) (due to a complicated argument involving degrees of freedom and because both \(\bar{x}\) and \(s\) are determined from \({\hat{p}}\)). \[\text{CI boundaries} = \bar{x} \pm \frac{2s}{\sqrt{n}}\]

    This formula can also be rewritten in terms of \(\hat{p}\). \[\text{CI boundaries (proportion)} = \hat{p} \pm 2 \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}\] Calculate the confidence-interval boundaries.

    The confidence interval is [, ].



    Solution

    First calculate the sample standard deviation. \(s = \sqrt{0.831(1-0.831)} = 0.3747519\).

    • Lower bound = \(0.831-2(0.3747519)/\sqrt{243} ~=~ 0.783\)
    • Upper bound = \(0.831+2(0.3747519)/\sqrt{243} ~=~ 0.879\)

    \[\left[0.783,\, 0.879\right] \]

    This formula can also be rewritten in terms of \(\hat{p}\), since \(\bar{x}=\hat{p}\) and \(s=\sqrt{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}\). \[\text{CI boundaries (proportion)} = \hat{p} \pm 2 \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}\]

  6. Question

    A scientist collects a sample (with sample size \(n=108\)) from a population. Each individual’s weight is measured, and summary statistics are calculated: \(\bar{x}=72.4\) and \(s=17.9\). The scientist calculates a 95% confidence interval for the mean weight (in grams).

    \[\text{confidence interval} = \left[69,\, 75.8\right] \]

    A correct interpretation is:

    ##ANSWER1##


    1. The boundaries are determined by a process that will place \(\bar{x}\) between the boundaries 95% of the time.
    2. When \(\mu\) is measured repeatedly, it will be between these boundaries (69 and 75.8) approximately 95% of the time.
    3. If the entire process of gathering the sample and calculating the confidence interval is repeated, there is a 95% chance the new sample mean will be between 69 and 75.8.
    4. If the same population were sampled repeatedly, and each time a confidence interval was calculated, then the population mean will be between the boundaries approximately 95% of the time.

    Solution

    Check the meaning of confidence interval.

    Notice the meaning is quite subtle and confusing. Most importantly a confidence interval allows us to indicate our uncertainty of \(\mu\). We have a pretty good guess (\(\mu \approx \bar{x}\)), and we have an idea that it’s probably not further off than the margin of error (\(\text{ME}\approx \frac{2s}{\sqrt{n}}\)).
