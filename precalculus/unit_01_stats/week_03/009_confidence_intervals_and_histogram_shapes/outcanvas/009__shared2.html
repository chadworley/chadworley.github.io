  2. Question

    A scientist has weighed 126 specimens of a newly discovered organism. Those weights have a sample mean equaling \(\bar{x} =48.3\) grams and a sample standard deviation equaling \(s=16.1\) grams. The scientist hopes to construct a 95% confidence interval of the organism’s population mean (\(\mu\)).

    The scientist will later consult a statistician for a more precise method, but for now she will use a quick method to estimate the 95% confidence interval: \[\text{CI boundaries} = \bar{x}\pm \frac{2s}{\sqrt{n}} \]

    The lower boundary is .

    The upper boundary is .



    Solution

    Plug the numbers into the expressions.

    • Lower bound = \(48.3-2(16.1)/\sqrt{126} ~=~ 45.4\)
    • Upper bound = \(48.3+2(16.1)/\sqrt{126} ~=~ 51.2\)

    Using interval notation, the 95% confidence interval is [45.4, 51.2].

  4. Question

    A pollster is trying to determine the proportion of likely voters who are still undecided (note: in Statistics, the definition of “proportion” is distinct from other definitions; it refers to a quotient of counts, part over whole, giving a number between 0 and 1.) They randomly select 61 likely voters, and in that sample 65.6% are still undecided. The pollster wishes to calculate the boundaries of a confidence interval of the proportion.

    Proportions are a special case: an average of 0s (not undecided) and 1s (undecided). In this special case, the single parameter (sample proportion \(\hat{p}=0.656\)) determines the sample mean \(\bar{x}\) and the sample standard deviation \(s\).

    \[\bar{x} = \hat{p}\]

    \[s = \sqrt{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})} \]

    The approximate formula to calculate the boundaries of a confidence interval remains the same, except Bessel’s correction is not used to calculate \(s\) (due to a complicated argument involving degrees of freedom and because both \(\bar{x}\) and \(s\) are determined from \({\hat{p}}\)). \[\text{CI boundaries} = \bar{x} \pm \frac{2s}{\sqrt{n}}\]

    This formula can also be rewritten in terms of \(\hat{p}\). \[\text{CI boundaries (proportion)} = \hat{p} \pm 2 \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}\] Calculate the confidence-interval boundaries.

    The confidence interval is [, ].



    Solution

    First calculate the sample standard deviation. \(s = \sqrt{0.656(1-0.656)} = 0.4750411\).

    • Lower bound = \(0.656-2(0.4750411)/\sqrt{61} ~=~ 0.534\)
    • Upper bound = \(0.656+2(0.4750411)/\sqrt{61} ~=~ 0.778\)

    \[\left[0.534,\, 0.778\right] \]

    This formula can also be rewritten in terms of \(\hat{p}\), since \(\bar{x}=\hat{p}\) and \(s=\sqrt{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}\). \[\text{CI boundaries (proportion)} = \hat{p} \pm 2 \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}\]

  6. Question

    A scientist collects a sample (with sample size \(n=189\)) from a population. Each individual’s weight is measured, and summary statistics are calculated: \(\bar{x}=32.7\) and \(s=9.3\). The scientist calculates a 95% confidence interval for the mean weight (in grams).

    \[\text{confidence interval} = \left[31.3,\, 34.1\right] \]

    A correct interpretation is:

    ##ANSWER1##


    1. The population mean \(\mu\) is between the boundaries.
    2. If the entire process of gathering the sample and calculating the confidence interval is repeated, there is a 95% chance that \(\mu\) will be between the new boundaries.
    3. There is a 95% chance that \(\bar{x}\) is between the boundaries.
    4. When \(\mu\) is measured repeatedly, it will be between these boundaries (31.3 and 34.1) approximately 95% of the time.

    Solution

    Check the meaning of confidence interval.

    Notice the meaning is quite subtle and confusing. Most importantly a confidence interval allows us to indicate our uncertainty of \(\mu\). We have a pretty good guess (\(\mu \approx \bar{x}\)), and we have an idea that it’s probably not further off than the margin of error (\(\text{ME}\approx \frac{2s}{\sqrt{n}}\)).
