  2. Question

    A scientist has weighed 96 specimens of a newly discovered organism. Those weights have a sample mean equaling \(\bar{x} =70.4\) grams and a sample standard deviation equaling \(s=15.7\) grams. The scientist hopes to construct a 95% confidence interval of the organism’s population mean (\(\mu\)).

    The scientist will later consult a statistician for a more precise method, but for now she will use a quick method to estimate the 95% confidence interval: \[\text{CI boundaries} = \bar{x}\pm \frac{2s}{\sqrt{n}} \]

    The lower boundary is .

    The upper boundary is .



    Solution

    Plug the numbers into the expressions.

    • Lower bound = \(70.4-2(15.7)/\sqrt{96} ~=~ 67.2\)
    • Upper bound = \(70.4+2(15.7)/\sqrt{96} ~=~ 73.6\)

    Using interval notation, the 95% confidence interval is [67.2, 73.6].

  4. Question

    A pollster is trying to determine the proportion of likely voters who are still undecided (note: in Statistics, the definition of “proportion” is distinct from other definitions; it refers to a quotient of counts, part over whole, giving a number between 0 and 1.) They randomly select 151 likely voters, and in that sample 42.4% are still undecided. The pollster wishes to calculate the boundaries of a confidence interval of the proportion.

    Proportions are a special case: an average of 0s (not undecided) and 1s (undecided). In this special case, the single parameter (sample proportion \(\hat{p}=0.424\)) determines the sample mean \(\bar{x}\) and the sample standard deviation \(s\).

    \[\bar{x} = \hat{p}\]

    \[s = \sqrt{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})} \]

    The approximate formula to calculate the boundaries of a confidence interval remains the same, except Bessel’s correction is not used to calculate \(s\) (due to a complicated argument involving degrees of freedom and because both \(\bar{x}\) and \(s\) are determined from \({\hat{p}}\)). \[\text{CI boundaries} = \bar{x} \pm \frac{2s}{\sqrt{n}}\]

    This formula can also be rewritten in terms of \(\hat{p}\). \[\text{CI boundaries (proportion)} = \hat{p} \pm 2 \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}\] Calculate the confidence-interval boundaries.

    The confidence interval is [, ].



    Solution

    First calculate the sample standard deviation. \(s = \sqrt{0.424(1-0.424)} = 0.4941902\).

    • Lower bound = \(0.424-2(0.4941902)/\sqrt{151} ~=~ 0.344\)
    • Upper bound = \(0.424+2(0.4941902)/\sqrt{151} ~=~ 0.504\)

    \[\left[0.344,\, 0.504\right] \]

    This formula can also be rewritten in terms of \(\hat{p}\), since \(\bar{x}=\hat{p}\) and \(s=\sqrt{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}\). \[\text{CI boundaries (proportion)} = \hat{p} \pm 2 \sqrt{\frac{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}{n}}\]

    Calculate the confidence-interval boundaries.

    The confidence interval is [, ].

    What conclusion will the pollster infer?

    .



    Solution

    Determine the sample size (use =count() in spreadsheet.) \[n = 63\]

    Calculate the sample proportion (use =average() in spreadsheet). \[\hat{p}=\frac{43}{63} = 0.6825397\]

    Calculate the standard deviation (use the algebraic formula \(s=\sqrt{\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})}\) or =stdevp() in spreadsheet.) Interestingly, we do not use the Bessel correction when calculating the sample standard deviation of a proportion. The reason has to do with the degrees of freedom.

    If the boundaries straddle 0.5, then the pollster needs more data. Otherwise, the pollster can predict the winner based on who has a higher sample proportion.


  6. Question

    A scientist collects a sample (with sample size \(n=88\)) from a population. Each individual’s weight is measured, and summary statistics are calculated: \(\bar{x}=48.3\) and \(s=11.8\). The scientist calculates a 95% confidence interval for the mean weight (in grams).

    \[\text{confidence interval} = \left[45.8,\, 50.8\right] \]

    A correct interpretation is:

    ##ANSWER1##


    1. If the entire process of gathering the sample and calculating the confidence interval is repeated, there is a 95% chance that \(\bar{x}\) will be between the new boundaries.
    2. There is a 95% chance that \(\bar{x}\) is between the boundaries.
    3. If the entire process of gathering the sample and calculating the confidence interval is repeated, there is a 95% chance that \(\mu\) will be between the new boundaries.
    4. There is a 95% chance that \(\mu\) is between 45.8 and 50.8. (Hint: this is wrong, but the reasoning is super subtle and slightly controversial.)

    Solution

    Check the meaning of confidence interval.

    Notice the meaning is quite subtle and confusing. Most importantly a confidence interval allows us to indicate our uncertainty of \(\mu\). We have a pretty good guess (\(\mu \approx \bar{x}\)), and we have an idea that it’s probably not further off than the margin of error (\(\text{ME}\approx \frac{2s}{\sqrt{n}}\)).
