Question

The graph of \(y=f(x)\) is shown below. Also, at \(x=2\), a tangent line is drawn.


The tangent line can be used to determine the instantaneous rate of change of \(f(x)\) at \(x=2\).

Please estimate \(f'(2)\) within the nearest hundredth. This could also be notated as \(\left.\frac{df(x)}{dx}\right|_{x=2}\) or as \(\frac{df}{dx}(2)\). For more, see wikipedia’s article on notation for differentiation.

Solution

Notice the tangent line intersects \(f(x)\) at point \((2,-2)\). From there, follow the red line until you get to another point with integer coordinates.


The slope of the tangent line is found with “rise over run”.

\[m ~=~ \frac{1}{4}\]

\[m ~=~ 0.25\]

So, the equation of the tangent line in point-slope form is \(y=0.25(x-(2))+(-2)\). More importantly for this question, the slope of the tangent line equals the instantaneous rate of change of the function, which equals the derivative at \(x=2\). In other words, \[f'(2) ~=~ 0.25\]

\[\left.\frac{df(x)}{dx}\right|_{x=2} ~=~ 0.25\]

\[\frac{df}{dx}(2) ~=~ 0.25\]
